\chapter{Theory of Optical Injection Locking}

\section{Why Remote Coherence Is Needed}

The central problem addressed in this dissertation is not only how to compute with light, but how to compute coherently with optical signals that are generated at different physical locations. Conventional networked computing treats optical links mainly as communication channels. A received optical signal is converted to an electrical waveform, digitized, processed electronically, and then possibly converted back into the optical domain. This workflow is reliable, but it discards the phase relationship carried by the optical field and introduces conversion penalties at every interface.

The proposed strategy is to preserve the useful optical phase reference across distance. A remotely modulated optical source is transmitted to a local processing node. Part of the received signal is used to injection-lock a local semiconductor laser, while another part is retained for later interference. The locked laser then acts as a local coherent carrier that can be independently remodulated with local data. Interference between the remote signal and the locally remodulated locked laser provides the physical multiplication mechanism required for coherent optical processing.

\section{Background and Development of Injection Locking}

Injection locking is a general synchronization phenomenon in which an oscillator locks its frequency and phase to an externally injected signal. The classical description traces back to Adler's analysis of weakly forced oscillators, where locking occurs when the detuning between the free-running oscillator and the injected signal lies within a finite locking interval \cite{adler1946study}. In optical systems, the same concept appears when light from a master laser is injected into a slave laser. If the injected field is strong enough and the detuning is sufficiently small, the slave laser follows the frequency and phase of the master.

For semiconductor lasers, injection locking has been studied for decades as both a fundamental nonlinear-dynamics problem and a practical tool \cite{lang1982injection,mogensen1985locking,liu2020optical}. It has been used for linewidth reduction, frequency stabilization, modulation bandwidth enhancement, coherent communication, carrier recovery, and neuromorphic photonic systems \cite{liu2015homodyne,lau2009enhanced}. These applications often exploit the fact that a relatively simple local laser can inherit the spectral reference of a remote or higher-quality optical source.

The use considered here differs from many communication-oriented uses of injection locking. In coherent communication, it may be desirable for modulation information to be preserved, transferred, or enhanced. In the distributed coherent computing architecture, the desired behavior is almost the opposite: the local laser should lock to the remote carrier while suppressing the modulation sidebands carried by the remote data. This is why the locking range must be large enough to track carrier-frequency variations, but not so broad or strongly driven that the local laser reproduces unwanted remote modulation components.

\section{Locking Range and Spectral Selectivity}

In a simplified oscillator picture, the locking range increases with injected field strength. For semiconductor lasers, the locking interval is also shaped by carrier dynamics and amplitude-phase coupling. A commonly used expression for the asymmetric semiconductor-laser locking range is
\begin{equation}
    -\kappa \sqrt{1+\alpha^2}\sqrt{\frac{P_{\mathrm{remote}}}{P_0}}
    < \Delta\omega <
    \kappa \sqrt{\frac{P_{\mathrm{remote}}}{P_0}},
    \label{eq:semiconductor_locking_range}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\omega$ is the angular-frequency detuning between the remote and injected lasers, $P_{\mathrm{remote}}/P_0$ is the injection ratio, $\kappa$ is the effective injection coupling coefficient, and $\alpha$ is the linewidth enhancement factor. This expression captures two important design facts. First, increasing injection power increases the detuning range over which locking can occur. Second, a nonzero $\alpha$ makes the locking range asymmetric because amplitude fluctuations are coupled into phase dynamics.

This spectral-selectivity requirement is the key physical constraint behind the simulation work. If the locking range is too narrow, practical detuning drift may break carrier locking. If the locking range or injection strength is too large, modulation sidebands and nonlinear relaxation-oscillation dynamics can contaminate the local carrier. The design goal is therefore not maximum injection strength, but an operating point that provides adequate carrier locking with minimal residual modulation transfer.

\section{Semiconductor Laser Rate Equations}

To analyze these trade-offs beyond the static locking-range expression, this dissertation uses a semiconductor-laser rate-equation model. The injected laser is represented by a slowly varying complex intracavity field $E(t)$ and a carrier number $N(t)$. The field evolution is
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dE}{dt}
    =
    \frac{1}{2}(1+i\alpha)G\!\left(N,|E|^2\right)E
    +
    \kappa E_{\mathrm{inj}}(t),
    \label{eq:field_rate_equation}
\end{equation}
and the carrier evolution is
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN}{dt}
    =
    R_p
    -
    \frac{N}{\tau_n}
    -
    g\!\left(N,|E|^2\right)|E|^2.
    \label{eq:carrier_rate_equation}
\end{equation}
Here $\alpha$ is the linewidth enhancement factor, $R_p$ is the pump term, $\tau_n$ is the carrier lifetime, $\kappa$ is the injection coefficient, and $E_{\mathrm{inj}}(t)$ is the injected remote-laser field. The gain is modeled with saturation as
\begin{equation}
    g\!\left(N,|E|^2\right)
    =
    \frac{g_0(N-N_0)}{1+s|E|^2},
    \label{eq:saturated_gain}
\end{equation}
with net gain
\begin{equation}
    G\!\left(N,|E|^2\right)
    =
    g\!\left(N,|E|^2\right)
    -
    \frac{1}{\tau_s}.
    \label{eq:net_gain}
\end{equation}
The parameters $g_0$, $N_0$, $s$, and $\tau_s$ denote the differential gain, transparency carrier number, gain saturation factor, and photon lifetime, respectively.

Equations~\ref{eq:field_rate_equation}--\ref{eq:net_gain} are the core of the theoretical paper's simulation framework. They make it possible to follow the time evolution of both the optical field and the carrier population after remote injection is applied. They also naturally include amplitude-phase coupling, carrier relaxation, gain saturation, and modulation-driven perturbations. These effects are exactly what determine whether the local laser behaves as a clean carrier-recovery element or as a nonlinear device that transfers unwanted remote modulation into the local processing path.

\section{Locking Criteria Used in Simulation}

For each injection ratio and detuning, the rate equations are integrated until the transient response has decayed. The remaining steady-state window is then evaluated using two metrics. The relative intensity swing is
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\Delta I}{\bar{I}}
    =
    \frac{I_{\max}-I_{\min}}{I_{\mathrm{mean}}},
    \qquad I(t)=|E(t)|^2,
    \label{eq:intensity_swing}
\end{equation}
and the phase excursion is
\begin{equation}
    \Delta\phi
    =
    \max\!\left(\phi_{\mathrm{unwrap}}\right)
    -
    \min\!\left(\phi_{\mathrm{unwrap}}\right),
    \qquad \phi(t)=\arg(E(t)).
    \label{eq:phase_excursion}
\end{equation}
In the theory paper, an operating point is treated as locked when both the residual intensity swing and phase excursion are small. This practical definition identifies the region where the injected laser is sufficiently stable to serve as a coherent local carrier for downstream computation.
