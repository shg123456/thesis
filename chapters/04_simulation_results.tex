\chapter{Simulation and Experimental Results}

\section{Locking-Range Mapping}

The simulation results first map the locking region as a function of injection ratio and detuning. These maps confirm that increasing the injection ratio generally broadens the carrier-level locking interval. When amplitude-phase coupling is included, the locking region becomes asymmetric, especially at stronger injection. This behavior agrees with the design intuition developed in the proposal: higher injection strength improves detuning tolerance but can also introduce more complicated nonlinear dynamics near the locking boundary.

\section{Experimental Locking-Range Validation}

The proposal-stage experiments validate the same locking-range picture using a tunable source laser, a local injected laser, an optical circulator, optical attenuation, and spectrum-domain measurements. In the unmodulated-source experiment, the source wavelength is swept through the target mode while the injected laser output is monitored. The locking range is identified from the region where the target mode dominates and the side-mode suppression ratio increases.

\section{Modulated Injection and Sideband Dynamics}

The modulated-source experiments address a more realistic operating condition in which the remote source carries data. A driven electro-optic modulator creates sidebands around the optical carrier before injection into the local laser. As the source wavelength is swept, either the carrier or its sidebands can fall into the locking range. This introduces additional sideband dynamics and motivates the simulation-based transfer-function analysis.

\section{Coherent Interference and Computation}

After injection locking is established, the local locked laser can be remodulated and interfered with the remote signal. The proposal-stage coherent interference experiments use a balanced detection architecture and a phase-stabilization loop to maintain a controllable interference condition. These experiments provide the experimental pathway from carrier recovery toward optical-domain multiplication and accumulation.
